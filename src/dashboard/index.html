Implication for Entrepreneurs
The pressure to write "perfect" code early on may be misplaced. The primary focus should be on building a high-velocity team and culture to demonstrate momentum; managing, rather than eliminating, technical debt is a secondary concern.
Implication for Investors
Technical due diligence may be less predictive of near-term success than metrics that capture a team's execution speed and iteration cycle. The composite velocity metric used in this study could serve as a useful proxy for a team's ability to deliver results and hit milestones.
Contribution to Theory
These findings challenge the universally negative view of technical debt in software engineering literature, adding nuance by demonstrating that in a resource-constrained venture context, debt can be a component of a successful, high-growth strategy.
Limitations of the Study
(1) Sampling Bias: The sample is limited to funded, open-source companies. (2) Proxy Variables: TDR is a model-based estimate and funding is a proxy for success. (3) Survivorship Bias: The dataset consists of companies that survived long enough to raise funding. (4) Causality: The study shows association, not causation.
